The Audience: What Makes Them React?

Posted by Shivali Chandra
May 13, 2016

To wrap up our rhetorical analysis, the last aspect of the debates we looked at was the audience. Throughout the transcripts from 1980 onwards, we were given some insight into how the audience reacted to each candidate through the applause and laughter that was transcribed. We were interested in looking at this further to see not just how many times each candidate could incite laughter or applause, but to see if we could somehow learn more about topics or phrases that typically generated these reactions.

In order to get these topics and phrases, we went through a series of steps. First, we use regex in order to locate the instance of laughter or applause. Then, we took the three sentences or statements prior to the reaction and saved them to the candidate in a dictionary. The regex searched for one to three sentences, so if there were fewer spoken by the candidate before the reaction a smaller amount would be recorded. The number three was mostly arbitrarily chosen; however, from reading through the debates, it seemed like three sentences was fairly good for only pulling the topics related to the reaction without getting sentences that were unrelated. The code for this task can be found here.

Additionally, we kept counts of the number of times each candidate generated a reaction and normalized the count by the number of debates they spoke in. Figures 1 and 2 display graphs of the average number of times certain candidates generated that particular reaction per debate. The candidates shown are the ones for whom the average count was not zero:

Interestingly, the candidate who generated the most amount of laughter was the only Independent candidate, Ross Perot. Searches on Google reveal that he was indeed very popular and well-liked due to his humorous streak which set him apart from other candidates. However, his second run for presidency in '96 was less successful --here, many of his debates are filled with his objections and appeals against interruptions, not humor, a fact which is revealed in our chart, as Ross Perot '96 is not present.

Another telling fact revealed by our data is that candidates who ran twice tend to have much higher average counts of laughter per debate, both their second time running and overall. This could corroborate the general theory that with more practice, they are more attuned to the audience and more at ease, making it easier for them to crack jokes or know how to make the audience laugh.

For some reason, this theory doesn't seem to be supported as strongly by the data we found for audience applause. Of the top candidates in this area, only a handful show up twice, and George H. W. Bush actually goes down in average count. The breakdown between Republicans and Democrats here is also quite intriguing - the first few highest counts are all Democrats, and the rest are nearly all Republicans. For laughter, the distribution is slightly more equal.

These observations combined together could indicate something about the nature of audience laughter versus applause. Judging by the correlation between repeat candidates and high scores, and the relatively even distribution between Republicans and Democrats, laughter could simply be an indication of how at ease a candidate is or how witty they are. On the other hand, the lack of correlation between repeat candidates and high scores, and the uneven distribution between Republicans and Democrats and even the years with higher scores could mean that applause is a better indication of how strongly topics being discussed resonante with the audience, or which candidates talk about issues important to the audience the most.

In order to see what candidates talked about to generate applause and laughter most commonly, we used the sentences relating to each instance (mentioned earlier in this post) in order to produce word clouds with frequently mentioned terms, after filtering out stopwords. Stopwords are words that are frequently mentioned but do not have any particular significance or defining feature, such as 'the' or 'and'. The code we wrote for this task can be found here. The word clouds are shown below in Figures 3 and 4:

As we suspected, the words here are largely words that hold no special significance to the election or presidential debate, such as 'question', 'right', 'yes', 'sure', or 'tonight'. In fact, many of these words suggest that the candidate was merely making simple jokes relating to questions or answers given.

The word cloud for applause, however, is much more meaningful. Here we can see words such as 'country', 'tax', 'america', 'children', and 'education'. The combination of these words with the inclusion of positive words such as 'believe', 'make', 'work', and 'better' indicate that candidates who deliver action-oriented speeches and answers resonate with the audience. The really fascinating conclusion that can be drawn here is that laughter can potentially help to indicate which candidates will perform better in polls, while applause, though it stems directly from what a candidate says, may not indicate candidate success so much as what the audience cares most strongly about.
